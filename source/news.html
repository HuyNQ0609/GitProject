A black hole , or black hole , is a region of space-time where the gravitational field is so strong that nothing—no
matter particles or even electromagnetic radiation like light— can escape it. General relativity predicts that
a sufficiently large amount of matter in a small enough range will distort spacetime to become a black hole.
Around the black hole is a surface defined by a mathematical equation called the event horizon , at which when
matter passes it will not be able to escape out of the black hole. The black hole is called a "black" because it
absorbs all the radiation and matter that pulls across the event horizon, like an absolute black body in
thermodynamics ; it's also not some kind of "hole" or "hole" but a region of spacetime that doesn't let anything out.
Quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that at the event horizon a black hole will emit radiation just
as a black body of a certain temperature emits thermal radiation. This temperature is inversely proportional to the
mass of the black hole, making it very difficult to observe this radiation for black holes of medium or stellar mass.
